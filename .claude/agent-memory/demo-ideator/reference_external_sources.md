## Feynman Lectures Vol. II
URL: https://feynmanlectures.caltech.edu/II_toc.html
Best for: deep mental models, Gauss's law geometric argument, field energy, dipoles.
Ch.1–5 are the canonical treatment of electrostatics; Ch.2 covers field geometry.
The "flux spreading over a sphere" argument for the inverse-square law (Feynman II Ch.4) is the gold standard for that demo idea.

## Griffiths Introduction to Electrodynamics (4e)
Best for: rigorous formulas, parallel-plate derivation via Gauss pillbox, superposition.
Ch.2 covers Coulomb, field, Gauss. The Gauss pillbox for parallel plates is in §2.5.

## OpenStax University Physics Vol. 2
URL: https://openstax.org/books/university-physics-volume-2/
Best for: canonical pedagogy, worked examples with real numbers, parallel-plate geometry.
Chapter 5 (Electric Charges and Fields) and Chapter 6 (Gauss's Law) are most relevant.

## PhET Interactive Simulations — "Charges and Fields"
URL: https://phet.colorado.edu/en/simulations/charges-and-fields
Best for: UX patterns for draggable charges, field arrow grids, equipotential drawing.
The PhET sim allows placing/removing charges and seeing continuous field update — our EquipotentialsDemo already captures this pattern.

## HyperPhysics (Georgia State)
URL: http://hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu/hbase/hframe.html
Best for: concept maps linking Gauss's law, parallel plates, conductor shielding.
Parallel plates and Gauss's law diagrams are particularly clear here.

## Falstad EM Applets
URL: https://www.falstad.com/emstatic/
Best for: continuous 2D field maps with charge placement. Good reference for what a saturated visualization looks like and why our log-opacity approach is better.

## 3Blue1Brown
Best for: visual metaphors that have gone viral — especially "divergence and curl" video
(https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=rB83DpBJQsE) for flux intuition, and the "Essence of linear algebra" series for vector field intuition.

## Steve Mould
Best for: tactile / physical intuitions — his charged balloon videos show the kind of concrete hook the textbook opens with. Useful for calibrating what a "WOW moment" actually looks like to a non-expert.
