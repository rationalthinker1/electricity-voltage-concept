A short essay on electricity

The field is the thing.

Almost everything you were told about electricity is built around a wrong picture — electrons rushing through a wire like water in a pipe. The real story, the one Maxwell wrote down and Poynting finished, is stranger and more beautiful. Electrons crawl. Energy sprints. And it doesn't travel inside the wire at all.

01 — Voltage

Voltage isn't pressure. It's a difference.

A voltage doesn't exist at a point. It exists between two points — a gradient in something called the electric potential. Think of altitude on a map, not pressure in a tube.

Pick any two points in space. If a positive test charge would do work going from one to the other, you have a potential difference between them. That difference, measured in volts, is the integral of the electric field along the path. The voltage is a property of the field, not the wire.

Connect a battery and the chemistry inside it pulls electrons to one terminal and shoves them out the other. That asymmetry sets up an electric field everywhere — not just inside the wire, but in the air, the table, the room. The field lines decay with distance, but they are there.

A volt is what you get when one joule of energy is required to move one coulomb of charge between two points. Nothing is “in” the wire. The wire is just the path the charge takes through a field that already exists.

02 — The Two Speeds

Electrons crawl. The signal flies.

Flip a light switch and the bulb turns on instantly. That is not because electrons rushed from the switch to the filament. They didn't. They barely moved.

0.02

A snail moves about 50× faster. To traverse a meter of wire, an individual electron takes ~14 hours.
Signal Propagation
~2×108
m/s · about ⅔ the speed of light in vacuum
The electromagnetic disturbance — the field — reaches the far end of the wire almost immediately.
The Ratio
1013

The signal beats the electron by ten trillion to one. They are not even the same kind of thing.

03 — The Poynting Vector

The energy lives outside the wire.

This is the part that makes physics teachers visibly uncomfortable. The energy from a battery to a lightbulb does not travel through the copper. It travels through the empty space around it.

In 1884, the physicist John Henry Poynting worked out where the energy in an electromagnetic field actually is, and where it goes. His answer is a vector pointing in the direction of energy flow at every point in space:

S = (1/μ0) E × B

For any current-carrying wire, the electric field E runs along the conductor (it has to, to push the charge), and the magnetic field B circles around it. Their cross product points radially inward, into the wire, all along its length. Energy is flowing into the wire from every direction at once.

The wire isn't carrying the energy. The wire is the destination. Energy pours in through the field surrounding it, gets absorbed, and is converted to heat or light wherever the resistance is.
