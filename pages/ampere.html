The magnetic Gauss

Gauss's law for electricity says: pick any closed surface, sum up the electric flux through it, and you get the charge inside divided by ε0. Ampère's law is the magnetic equivalent — with one geometric twist. Instead of a closed surface bounding a 3D region, you draw a closed loop bounding a 2D surface, and instead of flux you sum a line integral of B. The right-hand side counts current threading the loop, scaled by μ0.

The statement is exact, and it's a conservation law: no matter how the field bends or twists around the source, integrating it along a closed path picks out exactly the enclosed current. The geometry of B is constrained at the deepest level.

Why circles work

For a long straight wire, Biot–Savart tells you that B is tangent to circles centered on the wire and constant in magnitude on each circle. On the Amperian loop, then, the dot product B·dℓ is just |B|dℓ — the field is parallel to the path everywhere — and |B| comes out of the integral as a constant:

∮ B · dℓ = |B| · 2πr = μ0 I

Solve for |B|:

|B| = μ0 I / (2πr)

That's the entire derivation. One line, no integrals to evaluate. Compare with Biot–Savart's machinery and the saving is obvious.

Symmetry buys you everything

The same trick that made Gauss easy for spheres and infinite planes makes Ampère easy for cylinders and solenoids. The list of geometries where you can pull B straight out of the integral is small: long straight wires (circular loops), infinite solenoids (rectangular loops along the axis), toroidal coils (circles inside the toroid). Each rests on a single symmetry argument that tells you where B points and on which surface it's uniform.

There is no magnetic charge. There is only enclosed current — and the geometry it forces on the field.

When symmetry is broken — bent wires, finite solenoids, off-axis points — Ampère's law still holds, but you can no longer pull B outside the integral. You're left with a non-trivial line integral, and Biot–Savart becomes the easier tool.

What "enclosed" means

The right-hand side is the net current piercing any surface bounded by your Amperian loop. Push the surface around like a soap bubble; the count is invariant. A wire carrying I in one direction adds +I; a wire carrying I back adds −I; the pair cancels. A single wire threading the loop twice (it enters, leaves, enters again, leaves again — a closed loop on the other side of the bundle) gets counted twice. The slider above lets you stack up to five parallel wires; Ienc rises in lockstep with the count, and so does |B| at the Amperian radius.

Maxwell's correction

The form above is the static Ampère's law. Maxwell discovered that it's incomplete: if the electric field itself is changing in time, you have to add a term μ0ε0 dE/dt — the displacement current. With that correction in place, the equation says that magnetic field circulates around both real current and changing E. That single term is what makes electromagnetic waves possible: oscillating E and B regenerate each other, propagating outward at c. For everything in this lab, currents are steady and the correction vanishes — but it's lurking behind the next chapter.
