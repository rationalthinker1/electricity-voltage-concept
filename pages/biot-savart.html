Build a field from pieces

Biot–Savart is to magnetism what Coulomb's law is to electricity — but with a cross product. Every infinitesimal piece of current dℓ contributes a tiny field dB at every point in space, falling off like 1/r², and perpendicular to both the current and the line from the segment to the field point.

Curl the fingers of your right hand from dℓ toward the radius vector r̂; your thumb points along dB. That cross product is what makes magnetic field lines wrap around the current rather than radiating outward.

Currents do not radiate force outward like charges. They circulate it.

The math, in stages

The full statement:

dB = (μ0 I / 4π) · (dℓ × r̂) / r2

The constant out front, μ0/4π ≈ 10−7 T·m/A, is the magnetic Coulomb constant. The cross product dℓ × r̂ packages the geometry: only the component of dℓ perpendicular to r̂ contributes, and the result is perpendicular to both. The 1/r² is the familiar inverse-square fall-off, the same one you saw with point charges.

For a finite straight segment of length L, measured perpendicular from its midpoint at distance d, the integral can be done by hand. The closed form is:

|B| = (μ0 I / 4π d) · L / √(d² + L²/4)

Take L → ∞ and the second factor approaches 2, recovering the famous infinite-wire result:

B∞ = μ0 I / (2π d)

Why a cross product?

The cross product isn't an arbitrary mathematical choice — it falls out of the physics. The force on a moving charge in a magnetic field is F = q v × B. For Newton's third law to hold between two current-carrying wires, and for the force-on-current law F = Iℓ × B to be consistent, B itself has to come out of a cross product of the source current with the radius vector.

So the rotational character of magnetism is built in at the source. Electric fields point away from charges; magnetic fields curl around currents. Two different rules for two different geometries.

When the segment is long enough to act infinite

The slider above lets you crank d against L. Near the segment (small d, big L), the geometry looks essentially like an infinite wire and |B|/B∞ hugs 1. Far from a finite segment, however, you start to see the ends: the field falls off faster than 1/d, eventually like 1/d² because, far enough away, a finite segment looks like a point dipole.

That crossover — from "wire that goes on forever" to "tiny stub of current pointing somewhere" — is what the slider plays with. Most circuit calculations use the infinite-wire approximation, which is fine as long as you stay inside the segment's near zone.

Connection to special relativity

A static charge has only an electric field. A moving charge has both E and B. In a different inertial frame, the same charge may appear to move at a different speed, so the split between electric and magnetic looks different too. The total electromagnetic field is the invariant; B alone is frame-dependent.

Magnetism, in this sense, is the relativistic shadow of electricity. Biot–Savart is exactly what that shadow looks like for the steady, slow-current limit — the same regime as Coulomb's law for electrostatics. Maxwell stitched them together; Einstein explained why.
