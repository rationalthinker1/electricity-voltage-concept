A capacitor stores energy by separating charge across a gap. The field between the plates holds joules per cubic meter; integrated over the gap volume, the total stored energy is ½CV². Capacitance is just “how much charge can you stack at a given voltage.”

What “capacity” even means

A capacitor is a device whose voltage across its terminals rises in proportion to the charge you pile on its plates. The proportionality constant is its capacitance:

C = Q / V

Read it backward: it takes V volts of work per coulomb to add one more coulomb's worth of charges to the plates against the repulsion of the charges already there. Big capacitance means it costs little voltage to add charge — the plates accept a lot of Q before the voltage climbs. A farad is one coulomb per volt, which is so much charge per volt that real-world capacitors are usually picofarads (10−12 F) up to a few millifarads. Supercaps reach single-digit farads and feel slightly miraculous for it.

Parallel plates from Gauss

Two flat conductive plates of area A, separated by a gap d, with charge +Q on the top and −Q on the bottom. Draw a Gaussian pillbox enclosing the inner face of the top plate. Gauss's law gives:

E · A = σ A / ε0  ⇒  E = σ / ε0 = Q / (ε0 A)

Inside the gap, that field is uniform — constant magnitude, perpendicular to the plates, pointing from the + plate to the − one. The voltage between the plates is then:

V = E · d = Q d / (ε0 A)

Rearranging Q/V isolates the geometry-only piece:

C = ε0 A / d

Stick an insulator (a dielectric) into the gap and its molecules polarize: each dipole sets up a tiny counter-field that partially cancels the applied one. The net field drops by a factor εr, the relative permittivity, so the voltage drops by the same factor, so the capacitance climbs by it:

C = ε0 εr A / d

Energy: not in the plates, in the field

Build the charge up from zero. At intermediate charge q, the voltage is v(q) = q/C, and the work to shove a tiny dq from one plate to the other is dW = v dq. Integrate from 0 to Q:

U = ∫0Q (q / C) dq = Q² / (2C) = ½ C V²

Where does that energy actually live? It is distributed throughout the volume of the gap, at a density that depends only on the local field:

uE = ½ ε0 εr E²

Multiply by the gap volume A d and you recover the same ½CV² you got from the work integral. The plates don't store the energy — they only hold the boundary conditions. The energy lives in the field they create. This is the same idea that will run rampant in the Poynting lab two doors down: fields carry energy in their own right, not as accountancy tricks for the charges that source them.

Why dielectrics matter

Capacitance per unit volume is the only metric that matters when you're trying to fit thousands of capacitors onto a chip. Boost εr and you boost C without growing A. Water comes in around 80, common ceramics push past 1000, certain ferroelectrics flirt with 104. The catch is that all of these have voltage limits — push the field too high and the dielectric breaks down, ionizes, and arcs. The design space is a fight between εr, breakdown strength, and physical thinness.

Modern electrolytic and supercapacitor designs cheat the formula in a different way. They use enormously folded electrodes (carbon foam, etched aluminum) to crank up A, and they make the “gap” d a single molecular layer of electrolyte. The result: 1 F devices that fit in a coin cell.

A capacitor doesn't store charge. The plates always have +Q on one and −Q on the other, the total being zero. What's stored is the separation — and the field that fills the space because of it.

Three timescales

Hook a capacitor up through a resistor R to a battery V. The voltage on the cap approaches its asymptote as v(t) = V (1 − e−t/RC). Discharge it through the same resistor and it falls the same way. The product τ = RC is the time-constant: the fundamental memory of an analog electronic circuit. Every CMOS gate has one; every audio filter has one; every camera flash has one. The slider above doesn't show this directly, but every time you watch the C readout move, you're watching the time constant of any RC circuit you'd build with this cap move with it.

One last sanity check

The lab above shows the field between an idealized pair of plates — uniform, abruptly ending at the edges. Real plates have fringing fields that bulge outward near the rim. For aspect ratios where A ≫ d² (almost always, in practice), the fringing contribution to C is a small correction. The textbook formula gets you within a few percent.
