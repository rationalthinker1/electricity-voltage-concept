Chapter 1 · Lab 1.1 — Coulomb's Law

Two charges. One inverse-square law.

F = k Q₁ Q₂ / r²

The original electric-force equation, measured by Charles-Augustin de Coulomb in 1785 with a torsion balance so delicate it could resolve the twist of a silk thread. Same algebraic shape as Newton's gravity — and roughly forty orders of magnitude stronger.

The intuition first

Two static charges sitting in space. Same sign — they push apart. Opposite signs — they pull together. That much you already knew. The interesting part is the shape of the falloff. Move them twice as far apart and the force drops not by half but by a factor of four. Triple the distance, the force drops by nine. The exponent is exactly two, and it's exact because of a fact about space, not a fact about charge.

Picture each charge as the source of something radiating outward in every direction. By the time that “something” has reached a radius r, it has been smeared over the surface of a sphere of area 4πr². Whatever the local intensity is, it has to scale as the inverse of that area. Inverse-square is what you get when you push a finite amount of influence out through three-dimensional space.

The inverse-square law is not a fact about charge. It is a fact about space being three-dimensional. In a flatland universe, electricity would fall as 1/r. In four spatial dimensions, 1/r³.

The math, in stages

Coulomb wrote down what every careful measurement kept giving him:

F = k · Q₁ Q₂ / r²

The proportionality constant k ≈ 8.99 × 109 N·m²/C² is enormous. To get a feel for what that means, put one coulomb of positive charge on each side of an ordinary kitchen table — say one meter apart. The repulsive force is k · 1 · 1 / 1² ≈ 9 × 109 N. That's about ten billion newtons. Roughly the weight of a million tonnes — a stack of two hundred fully-loaded 747s on each end of the table.

No one experiences this in daily life because matter is electrically neutral to absurd precision. The electrons on one side of the table happen to balance the protons on the other to many decimal places. Subtract a fraction of a percent of that balance and you would blow the building apart. Static cling, lightning bolts, the entirety of chemistry — all of these are consequences of the residual, almost-perfectly-cancelled tiny fraction that ordinary objects can't keep balanced.

Why this form

The symmetric appearance of Q₁ and Q₂ in the numerator is forced by Newton's third law: the force on charge 1 from charge 2 must equal in magnitude and oppose in direction the force on 2 from 1. You can't write an asymmetric law without breaking reciprocity, so the dependence on the two charges has to be symmetric. Linearity in each charge separately is empirical — doubling one charge doubles the force — but it is the same statement, ultimately, as the superposition principle that runs all of classical electromagnetism.

The inverse-square dependence on distance is harder. Coulomb measured it directly with his torsion balance; Henry Cavendish had measured it a decade earlier (and never published); and modern experiments using nested conducting spheres place the exponent at 2 ± 10−16. It is the most precisely tested numerical exponent in all of physics.

The deepest reason it is exactly two comes from Gauss's law, which is the subject of Lab 1.3. If the integral of E over any closed surface depends only on the charge enclosed — in other words, if field lines don't terminate in empty space — then in three dimensions the field of a point charge must fall as 1/r². The 2 in the exponent is a consequence of the 3 in “three spatial dimensions.”

Compared to gravity

Newton's law of gravity has identical algebraic structure: F = G m₁ m₂ / r². The exponents match, the symmetry matches. Only the constants and the “charges” differ. The ratio is staggering. For two electrons sitting at any separation, the electric repulsion divided by the gravitational attraction is independent of r and equals

Fe / Fg = k e² / (G me²) ≈ 4.17 × 1042

Forty-two orders of magnitude. Yet the planet you're standing on is held together by gravity, not by electricity. That's only possible because the electric charges of ordinary matter cancel out to within parts per billion-of-billions, while masses can only ever add. Cancellation is everything — and the reason there is anything bigger than a small rock.

What εr does

Submerge the two charges in a polarizable medium — water, glass, oil — and the force between them drops. The medium's molecules are slightly off-center electrical dipoles; they orient against the imposed field and produce a smaller counter-field that opposes it everywhere. The net effect is a reduced effective field, and Coulomb's law gets a divisor: F = kQ₁Q₂/(εr r²). For water at room temperature, εr ≈ 80. Push the slider up and watch the force collapse. This is precisely why salts dissociate in water: the attraction holding Na+ to Cl− drops by a factor of eighty the moment the ion pair gets wet, and thermal jostling does the rest.
