The bizarre slowness

A snail crawls at roughly 1 mm/s. In a 12-gauge household copper wire carrying 20 amps, a typical free electron is drifting at about 0.02 mm/s — about fifty times slower than a snail. Crank the lab above to copper, 20 A, 2.5 mm²: the readout confirms it.

And yet, when you flip a light switch, the bulb illuminates instantly. The electrons in the filament don't have to wait for any other electrons to arrive. They were already there. The signal — the electromagnetic disturbance — propagated to them at roughly two-thirds the speed of light, in nanoseconds.

Three speeds live inside the same wire at once: drift (millimeters per second), thermal (a million meters per second), and signal (a hundred million meters per second). They are spread across fourteen orders of magnitude.

The math, with numbers

The relation is simple. If n free electrons sit in every cubic meter, each carrying charge q, all drifting with mean speed vd through a cross-section A, then the total current is just the charge per second crossing that section:

I = n q vd A  ⇒  vd = I / (n q A)

Plug in numbers for copper at 1 A through 2.5 mm²:

vd = 1 / (8.5×1028 · 1.6×10−19 · 2.5×10−6) ≈ 2.9×10−5 m/s

About 0.03 millimeters per second. To traverse a one-meter wire, an electron needs about 9 hours. At household currents in heavier-gauge wire, it's closer to 14 hours per meter. Per meter.

The reason is in the denominator: n is colossal. Copper has roughly one free electron per atom, around 8.5×1028 of them per cubic meter. The current is the product of that many particles and the drift speed. To carry a current you don't need to push them fast — you have a vast army moving at a crawl.

Thermal vs. drift — the two velocities

Inside the same wire, those same electrons are also moving at roughly 106 m/s. This isn't classical thermal motion (kT/m gives only ~105 m/s for an electron); it's the Fermi velocity, set by quantum mechanics. The conduction electrons occupy states up to the Fermi level, which corresponds to a typical speed of about 1.6 million meters per second in copper.

So picture this. A million-meter-per-second swarm in every direction at once. The applied field nudges this entire swarm with a slight bias of three hundredths of a millimeter per second. The bias is the current. The swarm itself doesn't go anywhere — it just leans, very slightly, downstream.

Why the bulb turns on instantly

Because the signal isn't any of the electrons. It's the electromagnetic disturbance — the changing E and B fields — propagating through the space around the conductor at near-c. When the field arrives at a given segment of the filament, the electrons there begin drifting. They were already in place. Nothing had to travel the length of the wire for the bulb to light.

Hit the lab's "Time to walk 1 m" readout for any household-scale current. It's hours. Now compare it to the signal-to-drift ratio in the readout panel below: usually around 1012. The signal beats the electron by a factor of a trillion.

The water-in-pipe analogy fails

In a water pipe, the water you turn on at the tap really did flow end-to-end through the pipe to get there. There's no equivalent for electrons. A better mechanical picture is an incompressible line of marbles preloaded into a tube: push one in at one end, one falls out the other end immediately, but no individual marble traveled the length of the tube. Current is like that, except the marbles were already touching each other (the conductor was already full of mobile charge) and the “push” isn't mechanical contact — it's the electromagnetic field, which arrived at every marble at once.

Connection to Ohm's law

In the Drude model, an electron between collisions accelerates under the field with a = qE/m, gains velocity for an average time τ, then loses memory of it on the next collision. The mean velocity that survives is:

vd = (qτ/m) E

Drift is linear in E, which is why current is linear in voltage. If the collisions weren't there, electrons would accelerate freely — current would grow as t². The reason Ohm's law works is that τ is brutally short — only about 2×10−14 s in copper at room temperature. Roughly fifty trillion velocity resets per second. Drift is the equilibrium between “getting pushed” and “forgetting you were pushed.”

So Ohm's law and the glacial drift speed are two faces of the same picture. The collisions make the response linear; the huge n makes the speed small. Nothing about a wire is fast except the field.
