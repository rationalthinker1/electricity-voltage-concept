From force to field

Coulomb's law tells you what happens when two charges meet. That works fine for two charges. But what about ten? Or 1023? Worse: how does charge 1 even “know” where charge 2 is in order to push on it? Action-at-a-distance was the philosophical wound Newton famously refused to suture. Faraday's answer, a century later, was to invent a new object: the field.

Instead of saying “Q₁ pulls Q₂ from afar,” say: Q₁ sets up a field E at every point in space, and Q₂ feels only the field at its own location. Action becomes local. Calculation gets cheap. And in dynamic situations — jiggling charges, radiating antennas — the field turns out to have an independent existence: it stores energy, carries momentum, propagates at the speed of light. The field is not bookkeeping; it's a thing.

The math

Define E as force per unit test charge:

E = F / qtest

Plug in Coulomb's law for the force from a point charge Q at distance r, take the limit of vanishing test charge (so the probe doesn't perturb the very field it's measuring), and you get

E = k Q / r²

The units are newtons per coulomb — which, after a one-line algebra check, equal volts per meter. They are literally the same unit, just measured differently: V/m is what you get if you integrate N/C over a length and divide by charge. The duality between “field strength” and “potential per length” is built into the SI definitions.

E is a vector. For a positive source charge it points radially outward at every point in space; for a negative source it points radially inward. Bring in a positive test charge and the force on it is along E. Bring in a negative test charge and the force is anti-parallel to E. Same field, different responses depending on the sign of the probe.

Why a field at all?

Coulomb wrote down a static law: charges sitting still. But what happens when Q₁ suddenly moves? Does Q₂ feel the change instantaneously? Einstein's answer was no — information cannot travel faster than light. The field formalism makes this answer automatic. E at a point right now depends on the configuration of charges in that point's past light cone. If a source jiggles, the news of the jiggle ripples outward through the field at speed c.

A field assigns a force-per-unit-charge to every point in empty space. The “empty” is wrong; the space is full of possibilities — a force that would appear the moment any test charge arrived.

The static E = kQ/r² you see in the lab is the steady-state limit of this much richer story. It's exact when nothing is moving, and it's a very good approximation when things move slowly enough that retardation effects don't matter.

Superposition

Coulomb's law is linear in Q, which means the field formalism inherits a fantastic property: the total field from many charges is the vector sum of the individual fields. There's no interaction term, no “Q₁ interferes with Q₂'s field.” Each charge contributes independently and the contributions add.

Etotal(r) = Σi k Qi (r − ri) / |r − ri|³

The whole of classical electrostatics is the practical exercise of evaluating this sum — or its integral form for continuous distributions — for various charge configurations. Two charges? You get a dipole field. A line of charges? Cylindrical symmetry. A surface? A sheet field. Volume? A bulk field. Same equation in different costumes.

Field lines aren't strings

The streaming orange lines in the visualization are a visualization, not a physical entity. Faraday drew them this way because they made the geometry obvious: density of lines indicates field strength, direction of lines indicates field direction. They begin on positive charges and end on negative charges (or run off to infinity in idealized open systems). They never cross — if they did, it would mean two distinct directions for E at one point, which is meaningless. Treat them as cartography. The real field is a continuous vector at every point.

The role of εr

Embedded in a polarizable medium, the source charge's field is partially screened by the medium's induced dipoles. The result is E = kQ/(εr r²) — same shape, scaled down. Slide it up to 80 (water) and watch the field collapse. The probe still feels something, but eighty times less than it would in vacuum.
