The fields are not bookkeeping devices — they are physical, energy-bearing entities. This is the foundation of the Poynting picture in the next lab.

Fields carry energy. Period.

You could, in principle, write all of electrostatics with no fields at all — just Coulomb's law and direct, instantaneous, action-at-a-distance forces between charges. The math would be ugly but consistent. The fields would seem like a bookkeeping convenience.

The trouble starts the moment you ask: where is the energy? Assemble a configuration of charges from infinity. You do positive work against their mutual repulsion. That work has to go somewhere. The charges themselves haven't changed — same mass, same rest energy. The only thing that's changed is the field. Maxwell, and then Heaviside and Poynting, did the bookkeeping carefully and arrived at an inescapable conclusion: the energy is in the field, distributed continuously throughout the volume of space it occupies.

Where the formulas come from

Take a parallel-plate capacitor. Charge it up. The total stored energy is U = ½CV². Substitute C = ε0 A/d and V = Ed:

U = ½ (ε0 A / d) (Ed)² = ½ ε0 E² · (A d)

That Ad is the volume of the region between the plates — the region where the field lives. So the energy per unit volume is:

uE = ½ ε0 E²

Same trick on an inductor's interior, using U = ½LI² and B = μ0nI:

uB = B² / (2μ0)

Both expressions only reference the local field strength. They make no mention of the charges or currents that source it. The claim is much stronger than what we showed: this energy density formula holds for any electromagnetic field, regardless of how or where it was set up.

The symmetry

Total electromagnetic energy density at a point in space:

u = ½ ε0 E² + B² / (2μ0)

In a plane electromagnetic wave propagating in vacuum, the two fields are locked together by Maxwell's equations: B = E/c. Plug this in and the two terms become identical. Half the wave's energy is in the electric field, half in the magnetic field, at every instant, at every point along the wave. Photons, you might say, are this energetic balance walking through space at the speed of light.

There is no such thing as “empty space” when there's a field there. Empty space is the cheap seats; field space is paying rent in joules per cubic meter.

Why the c factor matters when comparing E and B

The numerical asymmetry between E and B is dramatic. A field of 1 V/m — about what you get from a small static charge across a meter — carries an energy density of ε0/2 ≈ 4.4×10−12 J/m³. Meanwhile a magnetic field of 1 T — about a strong neodymium magnet — carries 1/(2μ0) ≈ 4×105 J/m³.

Seventeen orders of magnitude apart at the same numerical value. The reason is that E and B have different units. To get the same energy density, you'd need E = cB. A 1 T field is energetically equivalent to an electric field of 3×108 V/m — about a hundred times the breakdown strength of air. This is the lab's E* readout. It's also one way to see why visible light, with its wildly large c, ferries so much energy through space: modest fields, multiplied by an enormous c, become real power.

Comparing field energy to other things you've felt

Field energy densities are abstract until you start putting them next to chemical, mechanical, or thermal energy. The lab gives a rough comparison number with every slider position; here are the canonical anchor points:

CMB photon bath (everywhere in space):   ~4×10−14 J/m³
Earth's magnetic field (~50 µT):   ~10−3 J/m³
Capacitor at air's breakdown (3×106 V/m):   ~40 J/m³
MRI magnet at 3 T:   ~3.6×106 J/m³
Gasoline (chemical, for reference):   ~3.4×1010 J/m³

An MRI machine, in other words, has roughly a million joules per cubic meter sitting silently in its magnetic field at all times. That number explains why the bore is enormous, why the dewar is enormous, why every dropped wrench becomes a guided missile, and why quench events evaporate liters of helium in seconds: the field is a real, mechanical, energetic thing.

And in cosmology

Inflationary cosmology, the cosmic microwave background, dark energy — all are formulated in terms of energy densities of various sorts. The CMB photon density above (4×10−14 J/m³, averaged over every cubic meter of the observable universe) is what was left when the universe cooled enough that photons stopped scattering off plasma. It's tiny per cubic meter, vast in total. Same math, same formula, scaled to the whole sky.
