Changing field conjures voltage.

EMF = − dΦB / dt

A changing magnetic flux through a closed loop induces an electromotive force around that loop. Move the magnet, spin the coil, change the upstream current — the rule is the same. Every generator and transformer on Earth runs on this single equation.

The connection between magnetism and electricity

Up to this point in the chapter, all magnetic fields have been static. They sit in space; they push moving charges sideways; they do no work. Then in 1831, working independently of Joseph Henry across the Atlantic, Michael Faraday wound copper around two iron rings, connected one to a galvanometer, and pulsed a current through the other. The galvanometer twitched — not while the current flowed, but at the moment it switched on, and again the moment it switched off. Changing magnetic flux produces an electric field. That single observation created the entire electrical industry.

Pull a bar magnet through a coil of wire and a current flows in the wire. The voltage driving that current is the induced EMF, and its size is set by how fast the flux is changing through the loop. Spin a coil in a uniform field and the flux oscillates sinusoidally — you have a generator.

The math, in stages

The flux through a loop is the field strength projected onto the loop's normal, integrated over its area:

ΦB = ∫∫ B · dA

For a flat coil of area A in a uniform field, this reduces to Φ = BA cosθ where θ is the angle between B and the coil's normal. Wind N turns and the loops share a single flux path, so the total flux linkage is NΦ. If the coil spins at angular rate ω:

Φ(t) = N B A cos(ωt)

Faraday's law says the induced EMF is the negative time derivative of the flux:

EMF = − dΦ/dt = N B A ω sin(ωt)

Sinusoidal output whose peak is set by every input variable: N, B, A, ω. Crank any one and the amplitude of the scope trace above grows in proportion. The frequency you read off is f = ω/(2π).

The minus sign — Lenz's law

The minus sign in EMF = −dΦ/dt looks like a sign convention; it isn't. It encodes a real physical principle: the induced EMF drives a current whose own magnetic field opposes the change in flux that produced it. Push a magnet's north pole toward a coil, and the induced current organizes itself to push back — the coil becomes a north pole on the side facing the magnet. Pull the magnet away, and the induced current reverses to attract it.

This is energy conservation in disguise. You can't get electrical energy out of a generator for free. The mechanical work you do cranking the shaft against the magnetic resistance is exactly the electrical energy delivered to the load. Lenz's law is what makes the books balance. If the sign were positive, you'd have a perpetual motion machine on every workbench.

Magnetism never moves; only the flux does. Move the flux and electricity falls out.

What an AC generator is

A coil spinning at constant ω inside a constant magnetic field puts out a sine wave whose peak amplitude is NBAω. That equation is literally the design formula for an alternator. Wall-outlet power in North America is 60 Hz: that's a rotor turning 60 revolutions per second past stator coils. Set ω ≈ 377 rad/s in the slider and you'll see 60 Hz on the scope.

Hydroelectric plants spin coils with falling water. Steam plants spin them with high-pressure steam from boiling water (the heat source is the question — coal, gas, fission, geothermal, solar-thermal). Wind turbines drive them mechanically. In every case, the device that converts the kinetic energy to electricity is Faraday's law in a casing.

Transformers

Two coils share a magnetic flux path, usually wound on a common iron core. Drive AC into the primary; its time-varying current produces a time-varying flux. The secondary, sharing that flux, sees the same dΦ/dt — but its EMF is Ns/Np times the primary's, because flux linkage scales with turn count. Step a primary 120 V up to 12 kV with a 1:100 transformer and you can transmit power across hundreds of miles paying only the same I²R losses you'd have at a small current. Step it back down at the neighborhood substation to 240 V to be useful. The entire power grid is a single Faraday's-law equation, repeated thousands of times.

Maxwell's reformulation

In differential form, Faraday's law reads ∇ × E = −∂B/∂t: a changing magnetic field creates a curling electric field, even in empty space — no loop of wire required. That non-conservative E is what drives charges around in any loop you happen to wrap around it. Pair this with Maxwell's correction to Ampère's law and you have light: oscillating E regenerates B, oscillating B regenerates E, and the whole disturbance propagates at c.
