The mirror of capacitance

An inductor is the dual of a capacitor. A capacitor stores energy in an electric field between separated charges; an inductor stores energy in a magnetic field around moving charges. Where capacitance is the proportionality between charge and voltage, inductance is the proportionality between current and magnetic flux linkage. The two devices are so symmetric that even their differential equations swap:

IC = C dV/dt     VL = L dI/dt

Energy: ½CV² for the cap, ½LI² for the inductor. Voltage across one mirrors current through the other. If you've internalized the capacitor, you already half-understand the inductor.

Solenoid formula derivation

Take a long solenoid: N turns wound over a length ℓ, with cross-sectional area A, carrying current I. Define the turn density n = N/ℓ. Apply Ampère's law on a rectangular loop threaded through the coil — the field outside is negligible, the field inside is uniform and axial:

B = μ0 n I = μ0 (N/ℓ) I

The flux through one turn is Φ = B·A. Each of the N turns links that flux, so the total flux linkage is NΦ:

NΦ = N · μ0 (N/ℓ) I · A = μ0 N² A I / ℓ

Self-inductance is defined as the proportionality between current and flux linkage:

L = NΦ / I = μ0 N² A / ℓ

The N² is the magic. Doubling the turns doubles the field and doubles the linkages, so the inductance quadruples. Halving the length, holding N fixed, doubles n — same effect. This is why ferrite-cored inductors and tight winding patterns are everywhere in RF and power design: they buy you huge L cheaply.

Why current can't change instantly

Faraday says any change in flux through a loop induces an EMF that opposes the change (Lenz's law). For a self-inductor, the changing flux is caused by changing current in its own coil. The induced voltage that fights this is:

Vind = − L dI/dt

Try to interrupt current instantly — dI/dt → −∞ — and the inductor produces whatever voltage it takes to keep the current flowing for a moment longer. In practice, that voltage will arc across the open switch, ionize air, or destroy a nearby transistor. This isn't a quirk; it's the basis of spark plugs, flyback converters, and ignition coils. Every car still relies on it.

An inductor is a flywheel for electricity. It hates the new current. It loves the current you already gave it.

Energy in the magnetic field

Build the current up from 0 to I. At intermediate current i, the rate is di/dt and the back-EMF opposing your battery is L di/dt. Power going into the inductor is P = V·i = (L di/dt)·i. Integrate over the ramp:

U = ∫0I L i di = ½ L I²

Where does it live? Inside the solenoid, where the B field is. The energy density is:

uB = B² / (2μ0)

Multiply by the interior volume Aℓ, plug in B = μ0nI, and out pops ½LI² again — the same energy, written two different ways. As with the capacitor: the windings don't store the energy. The field inside them does.

The capacitor / inductor table

Side by side, every formula reflects across the diagonal:

Capacitor: V = Q/C,   I = C dV/dt,   U = ½CV²
Inductor:   I = Φ/L,   V = L dI/dt,   U = ½LI²

Couple one of each across the same wires and you get an LC oscillator: energy sloshes between the cap's E field and the inductor's B field at angular frequency ω = 1/√(LC). That's the heart of every radio.

RL time constant

Hook a series RL across a battery V. Current doesn't jump — it climbs:

I(t) = (V/R) · (1 − e−t R/L)

The time constant is τ = L/R. The lab's last readout shows what τ would be if you wired this inductor through a 1Ω resistor. Big L means a sluggish current rise; the inductor “remembers” the previous current for ~τ. RC and RL are duals here too: charges build with τ = RC, currents build with τ = L/R.
