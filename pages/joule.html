The energy bookkeeping

Move one coulomb of charge across a voltage drop of V volts and, by definition, you've moved V joules of electrical energy. Where did those joules go? Not into kinetic energy of the charge — the electrons exit the wire at the same drift speed they entered. Not into raising the charge's potential — the field lowered its potential by exactly V. They went into the lattice. Every Drude collision is a tiny inelastic transfer: the electron picked up kinetic energy between collisions, then dumped it as vibration when it next hit an ion.

The wire warms. Not because it stored anything — it didn't — but because it intercepted a continuous flow of energy coming in from the surrounding field (the Poynting flux of an earlier chapter), and converted it irreversibly into heat.

The wire doesn't store the energy. The wire intercepts it.

Three equivalent formulas

Power dissipated in a resistor is one number written three ways. From the energy-per-charge picture:

P = V · I

Each second, I coulombs cross the resistor, each losing V joules. Multiply. Now substitute V = IR:

P = I² R

The current-side form. Useful when you know what's flowing through. Or substitute I = V/R:

P = V² / R

The voltage-side form. Useful when you know what's applied. In a series chain the first form is convenient (same I everywhere); in parallel the second is (same V everywhere). All three are the same equation, useful in different setups.

Microscopic picture

The local form is even cleaner. Pick a tiny volume inside the wire. The rate at which the field does work on the moving charge per unit volume is the dot product:

pv = J · E = σ E² = J² / σ

Always positive (E and J point the same way in a resistor). Every cubic meter of conductor is converting σE² watts into heat. Integrate over the wire's volume LA, with E = V/L throughout:

P = σ (V/L)² · LA = σ A V² / L = V² / R

The macroscopic and microscopic accounts agree exactly. They have to: it's the same flow of energy, viewed at two zoom levels.

Why incandescent bulbs glow

The lab above starts in a tungsten-filament configuration. The filament is short (~5 cm), spectacularly thin (~0.001 mm²), and a poor conductor compared to copper. The resistance comes out a few ohms; at 12 V it carries a few amps and dissipates tens of watts. Skinny wire with a lot of power dumped into it has nowhere to send the energy except by glowing. The equilibrium temperature is set by Stefan–Boltzmann:

P = ε σSB Asurf T⁴

where ε is the emissivity (tungsten in vacuum ~0.4) and Asurf = 2πrL is the lateral surface area. Solve for T. With a real bulb's geometry you land near 2800 K. At that temperature, tungsten's blackbody spectrum overlaps the visible enough to produce useful illumination. About 5% of the input power escapes as visible light; the other 95% leaves as infrared and convective heat. Inefficient, but for a century it was unbeatable on cost and simplicity.

Why heaters are intentional resistors

Switch the material dropdown to nichrome. Its conductivity is roughly seventy times lower than copper's by design — it's an alloy chosen because it stays solid and chemically passive in air at red-hot temperatures (~1200 K), where copper would have melted and oxidized to powder. A typical toaster element is a length of nichrome ribbon sized to dissipate around a kilowatt at 120 V:

R ≈ V² / P = 120² / 1000 ≈ 14 Ω,   I ≈ 8.3 A

That's the design. Pick the cross-section and length to land on a resistance that gives the right power at line voltage, and the right operating temperature for whatever radiative or conductive transfer you want. Space heaters, hair dryers, stovetop coils, kettle elements, reflow ovens — same physics, different geometry. The resistor isn't a parasitic loss to engineer away. It's the entire product.

Limits of this picture

The lab above assumes steady state and treats resistivity as temperature-independent. Both are approximations. In reality, ρ rises substantially as a metal heats — tungsten's hot resistance is around ten times its cold value — so the cold filament grabs a big inrush current at the moment of switch-on (often the moment a bulb fails). The lab is also DC-only. At high frequencies (kHz, MHz, GHz), inductance and capacitance become significant, current crowds toward the wire surface (the skin effect), and the simple P = I²R bookkeeping needs additional terms. At 50/60 Hz line frequency these effects are tiny: a heating element really is, to excellent approximation, a pure resistor.
