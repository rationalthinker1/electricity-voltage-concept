What the cross product does

Of all the equations in classical electromagnetism, the Lorentz force is the one that tells you what E and B are for. They are defined operationally by the force they apply to a probe charge moving at velocity v:

F = q (E + v × B)

The first term is the familiar electric push: parallel to E, scaled by q. The second is the strange one. The cross product v × B is perpendicular to both v and B. So the magnetic force is always perpendicular to motion. A perpendicular force does no work. Kinetic energy is conserved; only direction changes.

Magnetism doesn't do work. It only steers.

Cyclotron motion

Set the pitch angle to 90° — v perpendicular to B — and the particle traces a perfect circle. Newton's law in the rotating frame: qvB = mv²/r. Solve for the radius:

r = mv / (|q|B)

And the period:

T = 2πm / (|q|B)

Notice what's missing on the right side: v. Faster particles trace bigger circles, but they take exactly the same time to go around. That speed-independence is the magic of the cyclotron. Ernest Lawrence built one at Berkeley in 1932 by exploiting it: a fixed-frequency RF voltage between two D-shaped electrodes accelerated protons every half-orbit; the protons spiraled outward at higher and higher energies but always returned to the gap right on time.

Add an E field

With both E and B, the dynamics get richer. A pure E in some direction adds a straight-line acceleration on top of the circular drift. Pair crossed E and B at right angles and you get a Wien filter: only particles satisfying qE = qvB → v = E/B pass through undeflected. The Hall probes, mass spectrometers, and electron-microscope columns of the 20th century are all variations on this geometry.

An electron gun in an old CRT television used a high-voltage E to accelerate electrons to a fraction of light speed and then a varying B (from deflection coils) to paint a raster across the phosphor screen. The picture you watched was Lorentz's formula traced 30 million times a second.

Why charged particles spiral around magnetic field lines

In a non-uniform B, the particle's motion decomposes naturally: the perpendicular component traces a tight circle (gyromotion); the parallel component is unaffected (no force from v∥ × B). The result is a helix wrapping the field line. Where the field strengthens, the gyroradius shrinks, the particle is repelled from regions of high |B| — the magnetic mirror.

That's why charged particles from the sun spiral down Earth's magnetic field lines to the poles instead of slamming into the equator: they ride the helix. The aurorae are excited oxygen and nitrogen, hit by gyrating electrons that have been mirrored back and forth thousands of times by Earth's field. Plasma physics — tokamaks, magnetic confinement fusion, Van Allen belts — is built top-to-bottom on this single picture.

The Hall effect

Run a current through a flat strip of metal and place a B perpendicular to it. The moving carriers feel a Lorentz force sideways. Charge piles up on one edge; a transverse voltage builds, called the Hall voltage. Its sign reveals the sign of the mobile carriers: positive for hole-conducting semiconductors, negative for ordinary metals. In 1879 Edwin Hall used this trick to prove that current in copper was carried by negative charges — decades before anyone knew what an electron was.
