The intuition

Picture a sled being pushed across snow. Push harder and it goes faster — but it doesn't keep accelerating forever, because friction bleeds off the gain. After a short startup, the sled settles at a steady speed proportional to the push. That is Ohm's law. The push is the electric field. The sled is an electron. The snow is the metal lattice it keeps colliding with.

Conductivity σ is how slick the snow is. Silver and copper are practically ice. Iron is gravelly slush. Nichrome is sand — designed to be barely conductive so that pushing current through it forces a lot of friction-heat into the wire. That's a toaster.

Conductivity is a single number that bundles up everything about how charges navigate a particular material — how many free charges there are, how heavy they are, how often they crash.

The math, in stages

A voltage V applied across a wire of length L sets up a roughly uniform electric field inside it. By definition, volts per meter:

E = V / L

That field accelerates charges. The microscopic Ohm's law says the resulting current density — current per unit cross-section — is just the field times the conductivity:

J = σ E

Integrate over the cross-section A and you get the total current:

I = J A

Put it together. I = σA·V/L. Re-arrange and the familiar macroscopic Ohm's law falls out:

V = I R,     R = L / (σ A)

The wire's resistance is its length divided by its conductivity-area product. Longer wire → more resistance. Fatter wire → less. Better conductor → less. That's it. The lab above is computing all four of these every time you wiggle a slider.

Why does this linear relationship even hold?

The Drude model (1900) gives the cleanest classical picture. Treat the free electrons in a metal as a gas zipping around at thermal speeds of order 106 m/s. Between collisions with the lattice ions — a mean time τ ≈ 2×10−14 s in copper at room temperature — the applied field accelerates them with acceleration a = qE/m. Each collision randomizes direction, so on average the velocity gained from the field is kept for a time τ before being scrambled. The drift velocity is therefore:

vd = (q τ / m) E

And since current density is J = n q vd (charge density times drift speed), we get the microscopic identity:

σ = n q² τ / m

Every term is a property of the material. n is how many free charges are around, τ is how long they survive between crashes, m is how hard they are to push. Bundle them up, get one number, call it σ.

The hard part: power, not energy, is the right thing to track

Every collision dumps the kinetic energy the electron picked up from the field as heat into the lattice. The rate at which this happens per unit volume is the dot product J·E = σE². Integrate over the wire volume and you get total power dissipated. The same number falls out as:

P = V I = V² / R = I² R

That's Joule heating. Notice in the lab: doubling V doubles I but quadruples P. This is why tungsten filament bulbs glow: tungsten's σ is low, so for any given current there's a substantial E along the wire, and all that I²R becomes thermal radiation hot enough to be visible.

About the material dropdown

Silver and copper are the kings of room-temperature conductivity (σ ≈ 6×107 S/m). Silver is marginally better but orders of magnitude more expensive, so all building wire is copper. Aluminum is about 60% as conductive but weighs a third as much, which is why power transmission lines are aluminum. Iron is roughly 6× worse than copper. Tungsten is worse still, but it stays solid at incandescent temperatures, so it makes filaments. Nichrome is intentionally bad — designed for heating elements, where you want high resistance per length so the wire dissipates significant power without melting.
