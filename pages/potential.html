The intuition first

Forget wires for a second. Imagine you're standing on a hillside. Pick two points. The height difference between them tells you something real: how much energy gravity will give you if you walk from the high one to the low one, or take from you if you walk uphill. That's it. That's the whole idea of potential.

Voltage is the same thing for charge. Pick two points. The voltage between them tells you how much energy the electric field will hand to a unit of positive charge as it moves from one to the other. A volt is a joule per coulomb. Move one coulomb between two points that differ by one volt and you've traded one joule of energy with the field.

Voltage is not a property of a place. It is a property of the path between two places in a field. There is no such thing as “the voltage here” without a reference point.

The math, said out loud

Start with the electric field E. It's a vector pointing in the direction a positive test charge would accelerate — in newtons per coulomb. So if you walk a tiny distance dℓ in the direction of the field, the force on a unit positive charge does work equal to E · dℓ on you. That's the dot product: only the component of E along your direction of motion counts.

Now walk from point a to point b. Add up E · dℓ along every step. That sum — the line integral — is the total work the field does on a unit positive charge. The voltage from a to b is the negative of that, by convention:

Vab = Vb − Va = −∫ab E · dℓ

Why the minus sign? Because V is defined to be high where positive charges want to leave. A positive charge gains kinetic energy moving from high V to low V. The field does positive work on it. So V drops in the direction of E. That minus sign keeps the bookkeeping straight.

Why the path doesn't matter

Here's the part that should seem strange: the line integral above gives the same answer no matter what path you take from a to b. Walk in a straight line. Walk in a spiral. Walk to the moon and back. Same number.

This is because the electrostatic field is conservative — its curl is zero. Mathematically, that means you can write it as the gradient of a single scalar function, which we call the electric potential V(x, y, z). Once such a function exists, the integral between any two points only depends on the endpoints, like climbing a hill. The total change in elevation between two cities doesn't depend on the route you drove. Same physics.

For a single point charge Q at the origin, the potential at distance r is the integral of E = kQ/r² from infinity inward:

V(r) = kQ / r

For many charges, the field is the vector sum of each one's contribution, so the potential is the scalar sum — which is much easier to compute. That's the formula running in the lab above. The voltage at any point is just the algebraic sum of kQ₁/r₁ and kQ₂/r₂, and the colors you see on the canvas are bands of constant V.

The hard part: why this is “voltage”

In a circuit, when we say “there's 9 volts across this battery,” we mean: between the two terminals, the field has organized itself such that one joule would be delivered per coulomb traveling from + to −. That field doesn't live inside the battery. The chemical reactions inside the battery maintain a charge separation; the resulting field fills the entire space around the circuit.

So when you measure 9V at a wall outlet, what you're really doing is dragging a test charge through space from one terminal to the other and counting the work. The voltmeter is doing it electronically, but the physical content is the same line integral that's drawn between probes A and B above. The wire just defines where the integration path most naturally lies.

What the εr slider does

In a dielectric (like water, glass, plastic), the molecules polarize in the presence of an external field. Their dipoles align and produce a small counter-field that reduces the net field everywhere. The total field gets divided by εr, the relative permittivity. So does the potential. Water has εr ≈ 80 at room temperature, which is why dissolved ions move around so freely in it. Slide it up and watch the voltage between A and B drop by the same factor.
