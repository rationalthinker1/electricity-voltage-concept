The intuition

You have to swallow a strange fact: the energy that lights the bulb is not inside the wire. It travels through the empty space around it, in the form of the electromagnetic field. The wire is the destination for energy, not the medium through which it travels.

In Volume II of his lectures, Feynman puts it in the bluntest possible terms:

“Since the wire has resistance, there is an electric field along it, driving the current… the E and B are at right angles; therefore there is a Poynting vector directed radially inward… there is a flow of energy into the wire all around.”

The wire's resistance is what couples energy out of the field and into heat. The energy itself was always streaming through the surrounding space. Every point of every wire in your house is being bathed in inward-flowing electromagnetic energy whenever current is on.

Building S from E × B

Two fields are present everywhere along a current-carrying wire. E is along the wire's axis — it has to be, because that's what drives the charge along the wire to make a current in the first place. B circles the wire (Ampère's law), curling counter-clockwise when seen from the direction the current is flowing.

The Poynting vector is their cross product, divided by the permeability of free space:

S = (1/μ0) E × B

Right-hand rule: axial E crossed with circumferential B produces a vector that points radially inward at the surface of the wire. Energy is entering, not leaving. This is the visual centerpiece above.

The integral that closes the loop

Inside a uniform resistive wire of length L with voltage drop V, the axial field is just:

E = V / L

At the wire's surface, where the radius is a, Ampère's law gives the magnetic field:

B = μ0 I / (2π a)

Multiply, divide by μ0, and you get the Poynting magnitude at the surface:

|S|surf = E B / μ0 = V I / (2π a L)

The wire's lateral surface area is 2πaL. So the total energy flowing in per second through that surface is:

∮ S · dA = |S| · 2πaL = V I

Exactly VI. The flux integral equals the dissipated power, identically, for every slider combination. The lab above is computing both sides and showing you their ratio — it stays at 1.000.

Davis & Kaplan, 2011

The 2D toy model above — a long straight wire with a uniform axial field — is exact, but a real circuit is curvier than that. Davis and Kaplan worked out the full 3D Poynting field around a circular loop of resistive wire driven by a battery. The result is exactly what you'd expect from energy conservation: field lines of S thread through every point of space surrounding the circuit, and the net flux entering the resistive portion of the wire equals the power dissipated there. The same flux leaves the battery, which acts as a source.

The picture is robust: in vacuum, with conductors only on the boundaries, energy is in the field. The wire is just where the field gives it up.

The superconductor limit

What happens as σ → ∞? The wire becomes a perfect conductor. The axial E inside must drop to zero (you can't sustain a field in a perfect conductor at equilibrium), so S = 0 inside. The energy doesn't enter at all. It keeps flowing, parallel to the wire, in the surrounding space. Nothing is lost. In a circuit with one resistor and superconducting leads, the entire energy flow happens through the resistor's surroundings — the leads are just guides for the field.

What about AC?

Fields oscillate, so S oscillates with them. The time-averaged Poynting vector still points into resistive loads, delivering real power. For purely reactive loads — ideal capacitors and inductors — the time-averaged S is zero. Energy sloshes in and out each cycle, with no net transfer.
