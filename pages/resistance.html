Chapter 3 · Lab 3.2 — Resistance

Length adds, area divides.

R = ρL / A = L / (σA)

The macroscopic Ohm's-law variable. Resistance is a property of the wire, not the field. Take a piece of any conductor and its resistance is what its length and cross-section conspire to be, weighted by the material's resistivity ρ. The microscopic J = σE is what's happening underneath; this is the lump.

From microscopic to macroscopic

A previous lab gave the local law: J = σE. At every point inside a conductor, the current density is the conductivity times the field, full stop. That's a statement about points. To wire up a circuit, you need a statement about wires: how much current flows when you connect a real, finite piece of metal to a real voltage source. That statement is V = IR, and the lump constant R is what the field-level law looks like after you integrate it over the geometry of the wire.

Resistance is the rendezvous of material and shape. The material brings its σ (or equivalently its resistivity ρ = 1/σ). The shape brings its length and its cross-section. Multiply them together the way the integrals demand, and you get a single number with units of ohms.

Resistance is what conductivity looks like after geometry has had its say.

Derivation, in stages

Imagine a straight wire of length L and uniform cross-section A connected to a battery of voltage V. Inside the wire, the field is roughly uniform along the axis (in steady state, surface charges redistribute to make this so):

E = V / L

Microscopic Ohm's law converts that field into a current density:

J = σ E = σ V / L

Total current is current density integrated over the cross-section. For uniform J, that's just I = JA:

I = σ A V / L

Rearrange to put V on one side:

V = (L / σA) · I = R · I

The bracketed term is the resistance. Substitute ρ = 1/σ (the resistivity is the inverse of conductivity, units of Ω·m) and you have the textbook form:

R = ρ L / A

Three numbers. A property of the material times a property of the shape. The whole macroscopic theory of DC circuits unfolds from that one line.

Why this geometry

Look at the formula and ask two physical questions. Why does L live in the numerator? Because length is the path the field drives the charges over. Double the length and you double the distance every coulomb has to travel under the same drag. For a fixed current, that means twice the voltage to push them along — resistance has doubled.

Why does A live in the denominator? Because area is the number of parallel paths. Doubling the cross-section means twice as many electrons can flow side-by-side, sharing the load. For the same current you only need half the current density, and so half the field, and so half the voltage. Resistance has halved.

The asymmetry between length and area is the whole reason wires are wires — long, thin, and made of copper. Power transmission engineers live on the area side of the equation. Heater designers live on the length side.

Series and parallel

The geometry argument scales up. Two resistors in series share the same current; the voltages add. So:

Rseries = R1 + R2

That's just the formula for a single longer wire, written for two pieces in a row. A long wire is many short wires in series.

Two resistors in parallel share the same voltage; the currents add. The arithmetic comes out as:

1 / Rparallel = 1 / R1 + 1 / R2

That's the formula for a single fatter wire, written for two pieces side-by-side. A fat wire is many thin wires in parallel. Series and parallel rules aren't separate from R = ρL/A — they are that formula, applied twice and rearranged.

Material is destiny

Try every entry in the material dropdown. The resistance numbers move by orders of magnitude. Copper, the workhorse, has ρ ≈ 1.68×10−8 Ω·m. Silver edges it out by a few percent. Aluminum is about 60% as conductive but a third of the density — the reason long-distance power lines are aluminum-cored rather than copper.

Tungsten is roughly six times worse than copper at room temperature, but the property that matters is that it stays solid above 3000 K. That's what lets it become a filament. Nichrome is intentionally, deliberately bad — about seventy times worse than copper — and that's why it ends up in toasters and space heaters. Resistance is the design variable, not the side effect.

Past the metals, the dynamic range opens up. Semiconductors range from about 10−3 to 103 Ω·m depending on doping. Glass and good ceramics hit 1012 to 1016 Ω·m. Superconductors below their critical temperature are exactly zero. The resistivity span across condensed matter is roughly twenty-four orders of magnitude. No other material parameter varies so violently.

Temperature dependence (mentioned, not modelled)

The numbers in the lab above are room-temperature. Real ρ is a function of temperature, and the sign of the dependence flips between conductors and insulators. In metals, lattice ions vibrate harder as T rises, so electrons scatter more, τ falls, σ falls, ρ rises. A glowing tungsten filament carries roughly ten times its cold resistance — which is also why incandescent bulbs draw a brief inrush current and tend to burn out at the moment of switch-on. In semiconductors and most insulators the dependence runs the other way: higher T liberates more carriers, ρ falls. The sign of dρ/dT is one of the tidiest experimental distinctions between a metal and an insulator.
